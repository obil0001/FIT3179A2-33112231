Introduction

This visualisation explores Victorian average taxation and earnings for the year 2014, showcasing different areas of Victoria and how they compare based on income and taxes.

Distribution of Victoria's Tax

This visualization provides a detailed look at how taxation levels vary across different suburbs in Victoria for the year 2014. Each area is color-coded to represent the average net tax paid by its residents. By examining this distribution, we can identify regions where tax contributions are higher and explore possible reasons, such as higher income brackets or property values in those areas. This insight helps in understanding the economic landscape of Victoria and how taxation impacts different suburbs. The closer to the CBD this visualisation gets the tax score also increases. We find that Toorak on average paid the most net tax to of $76,000, but also was one of victorias top earners, with its net income (after tax) still being $87,000+

Distribution of Victoria's Median Income

The second visualization illustrates the distribution of median income across Victoria. Suburbs with higher or lower median incomes are shown in different colors, offering a clear comparison of wealth across the region. This visualization is particularly useful for identifying socio-economic disparities and can prompt discussions about economic development and financial inequality within Victoria. It allows viewers to easily see where wealth is concentrated and how income levels might correspond to the tax burdens displayed in the first visualization.

Across Australia

  • Average Taxable Income or Loss: NSW and WA show the highest taxable incomes, exceeding $60,000, while states like Tasmania and South Australia have significantly lower averages, closer to $40,000.
  • Average Salary and Wages: The pattern here is similar, with WA showing the highest salaries at over $60,000, followed closely by NSW and NT. Lower wages are observed in Tasmania and South Australia.
  • Average Net Tax: WA again leads, with an average net tax of approximately $20,000, while other states such as Tasmania and South Australia report much lower figures, closer to $10,000.
  • In general, WA and NSW consistently show higher financial figures in all three categories, while TAS and SA tend to fall behind, indicating economic disparities across regions.
